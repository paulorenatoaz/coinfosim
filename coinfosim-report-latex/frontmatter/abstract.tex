\chapter*{ABSTRACT}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{ABSTRACT}

This study investigates the extent to which classifiers trained on synthetic samples, produced by generative models fitted to the real training data, preserve the predictive cooperation profile observed when the same classifiers are trained on real samples. The predictive cooperation profile of a set of information channels is the quantitative representation of how the relative predictive performance of channel subsets evolves with sample size, for a fixed dataset, classifier, and training condition; a predictive cooperation pattern is an interpreted regularity extracted from one or more profiles, not the quantitative object itself. The protocol compares three training conditions --- real training data, samples from a class-conditional single Gaussian, and samples from a Gaussian mixture model, both generative models fitted only to the real training data --- evaluated on the same fixed real test set. Every non-empty channel subset is examined over a grid of per-class sample sizes through Monte Carlo simulation with incrementally nested samples, using the estimated mean test loss as the comparison quantity between subsets. Experiments use the Occupancy Detection, UCI Air Quality, and SUPPORT2 datasets with linear SVM, Logistic Regression, Gaussian Naive Bayes, and, additionally for SUPPORT2, Random Forest. Profile preservation is evaluated through four complementary indicators that are never combined into a single index: ranking correlation ($\rankrho$) between complete subset orderings; \WinnersAgreement{} ($\AW$) between effective pairwise winner relations, obtained from a winner matrix ($\Wmat$) with deterministic carry-forward of exact ties; reversal existence agreement ($\AR$), which compares which unordered subset pairs underwent at least one valid winner reversal, recorded in an upper-triangular reversal matrix ($\Rmat$); and reversal sample-size similarity ($\SR$), conditional on shared reversal support, complemented by its unnormalized mean log2 reversal distance ($\DR$). Results obtained from currently available data show that profile preservation is multidimensional: synthetic training can reproduce the global ordering and the pairwise winner relations observed under real training without necessarily reproducing the same reversal pairs or the same sample-size regimes in which those reversals occur, so that $\rankrho$, $\AW$, $\AR$, and $\SR$ must be interpreted separately. Absolute predictive performance, geometric fidelity, and predictive-cooperation-profile preservation are therefore distinct empirical properties, dependent on dataset, classifier, generative model, and sample size. The study does not demonstrate cost reduction, nor does it establish a mutual-information relationship among channels; it instead provides reproducible formal objects --- $\Wmat$, $\Rmat$, and the four preservation indicators --- for future investigation of cost-efficient channel-selection strategies and of projections into larger sample-size regimes.
